Arborist Science Reference

ISA Best Management Practices · ANSI A300 Standards · Species Protocols · Cut Techniques

Crown Cleaning

Removal of dead, dying, diseased, crowded, weakly attached, and low-vigor branches from the crown. Preferred service for general health maintenance with minimal tree stress.

Crown Raising

Removal of lower branches to provide clearance for buildings, vehicles, pedestrians, and sight lines. Maintain live crown ratio ≥60% for most species after work.

Crown Reduction

Reduces overall size using natural reduction cuts (back to laterals ≥⅓ diameter of removed limb). Never a flat topping cut. ANSI-approved language: "selective reduction" or "end-weight reduction."

Crown Restoration

Improves structure, form, and appearance of trees with prior improper pruning. Multi-year program typically required. Refer to ISA-certified arborist.

Structural Pruning

Directed at young and maturing trees to reduce co-dominant stems, included bark, and poor branch architecture. High long-term ROI — prevents future hazard removals.

Clearance Pruning

Specific clearance from structures, utilities, or right-of-way. Utility work within 10 ft of energized lines requires OSHA-qualified line-clearance arborists only.

  • 25% Live Crown Rule: Never remove more than 25–30% of live crown in a single growing season for mature trees.
  • Branch Collar Cuts: Always cut just outside the branch collar — never flush, never into it. Contains protective chemical boundary tissue critical for CODIT response.
  • 3-Cut Method: Required for limbs >2 inches. (1) Undercut 12–18 in. from trunk; (2) Top cut outside undercut removes weight; (3) Final collar cut. Prevents bark stripping.
  • No Topping: Condemned by ISA, ANSI, and the National Arborist Association. Opens massive decay pathways, produces weak epicormic sprouts. Never use topping language in customer materials.
  • No Lion's Tailing: Stripping inner branches creates end-heavy, wind-failure-prone limbs. Always preserve inner foliage for structural load distribution.
  • No Wound Dressings: ISA research shows sealants impede natural compartmentalization. Exception: oak or elm cuts during beetle season.
  • CODIT Theory (Shigo 1977): Trees compartmentalize wounds — they do not heal. Four walling zones limit decay spread. Good collar cuts maximize CODIT response.
  • Root Zone Protection: Critical Root Zone (CRZ) = 1 ft radius per inch DBH minimum. Avoid compaction, grade changes, or excavation within CRZ.
  • Utility Clearance: Work within 10 ft of energized lines requires OSHA-qualified line-clearance arborists. Standard crews must not enter this zone.

✓ Natural Target Cut

Cut just outside the branch collar at the natural union angle. Gold standard for all branches. Maximizes CODIT response and wound closure rate.

✓ Reduction Cut (Lateral)

Cut back to a lateral branch at least ⅓ the diameter of the removed limb. Used for crown reduction. Maintains natural tree form. ANSI A300 Part 1 compliant.

✓ Three-Cut Method

For limbs >2 in.: (1) Undercut 12–18 in. from trunk; (2) Top cut outside undercut removes weight; (3) Final collar cut. Prevents bark stripping and tearing.

✗ Stub Cut (FORBIDDEN)

Leaving a stub outside the branch collar. Stubs die back and create major decay entry points. Always complete the cut at the collar on every branch removed.

✗ Flush Cut (FORBIDDEN)

Cutting into or through the branch collar. Destroys the protective chemical boundary. Results in severe decay column. Never flush-cut a living branch.

✗ Topping / Heading (FORBIDDEN)

Cutting back to an internodal stub. ISA-condemned. Creates epicormic sprout regrowth, massive decay, and structurally weak canopy architecture.

Failure Potential Indicators

  • Included bark at branch unions
  • Co-dominant stems with tight V-angles
  • Cracks, splits, or open wounds
  • Cavity or hollow sections
  • Fungal conks or mushroom growth at base
  • Severe lean — especially recent change
  • Root zone disturbance or soil heaving
  • Crown dieback or sudden epicormic sprouting

Target Assessment

  • House, roof, foundation, garage
  • Power lines & service drops
  • Gas, water, fiber, irrigation underground
  • Driveway, road, public sidewalk
  • Pool, hot tub, shed, outbuildings
  • Neighbor property or fencing
  • Public right-of-way exposure
  • Parked vehicles or equipment

ISA Risk Rating Levels

Low: No visible defects, good structure, low-value targets, open access.

Medium: Minor defects, moderate targets, some access constraints.

High: Significant defects, high-value targets, limited access, climbing or rigging likely.

Extreme / SVR: Utilities, storm damage, dead wood, large removals — site visit always required.

TRAQ Formal Assessment

ISA Tree Risk Assessment Qualification (TRAQ) is the industry standard for formal written risk assessments. Only TRAQ-qualified arborists may provide formal assessments. This tool performs preliminary screening only.

Small Tree / Shrub

Under ~20 ft

  • Hand pruners, loppers, hand saw
  • Pole pruner (telescoping)
  • Small chainsaw
  • Chip truck or debris trailer
  • Typical crew: 2–3 people, 1.5–3 hrs

Medium Tree

20–45 ft

  • Gas chainsaw (16–20 in. bar)
  • Pole saw
  • Aerial lift (if access available)
  • Climbing equipment
  • Chipper truck
  • Typical crew: 3–4 people, 3–5 hrs

Large Tree

45–70 ft

  • Gas chainsaw (20+ in. bar)
  • Aerial lift or climbing required
  • Rigging blocks, lowering lines
  • Large chipper truck
  • Log truck possible
  • Typical crew: 4–5 people, 5–8 hrs

Very Large / Hazard

70+ ft or high complexity

  • Large chainsaw, full climbing gear
  • Crane possible for section removal
  • Rigging systems, pulleys, blocks
  • Large chipper + log truck
  • Traffic control if near road
  • Typical crew: 5–6+ people, full day+
